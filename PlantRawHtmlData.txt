</h2>\n              <ul>\n               <li>\n                Watermelons can be grown on a trellis.\n               </li>\n               <li>\n                Use a trellis at least 8 x 8 feet (2.4m) wide or wider. Make sure the trellis is well-anchored.\n               </li>\n               <li>\n                Space plant at the base of the trellis 3 to 4 feet (.9-1.2m) apart.\n               </li>\n               <li>\n                Train vines up the trellis; secure the vines to the trellis with elastic garden tape.\n               </li>\n               <li>\n                Support melons growing on a trellis with netting. Nylon netting will easily support a whole watermelon if tied into a well-anchored support.\n               </li>\n               <li>\n                You can also grow watermelons on an A-frame trellis. move them outdoors when the weather has warmed but be careful not to pinch or break the vines.\n               </li>\n              </ul>\n              <h2 id=\"h-watering-watermelons\">\n               <span class=\"ez-toc-section\" id=\"Watering_Watermelons\">\n               </span>\n               <strong>\n                <img alt=\"Small watermelon fruit\" class=\"lazyload alignright size-medium wp-image-67855\" data-src=\"https://harvesttotable.com/wp-content/uploads/2009/03/Watermelon-bigstock-Small-Watermelon-In-The-Hand-318354763-300x225.jpg\" decoding=\"async\" height=\"225\" loading=\"lazy\" src=\"data:image/svg+xml;charset=utf-8,%3Csvg xmlns%3D'http%3A%2F%2Fwww.w3.org%2F2000%2Fsvg' viewBox%3D'0 0 300 225'%2F%3E\" width=\"300\"/>\n                Watering Watermelons\n               </strong>\n               <span class=\"ez-toc-section-end\">\n               </span>\n              </h2>\n              <ul>\n               <li>\n                Watermelons are 95 percent water. They require plentiful regular, even watering for quick growing.\n               </li>\n               <li>\n                Give watermelons 1 to 2 inches (2.5.-5cm) of water every week (1 inch equals 16 gallons/60.5 liters.)\n               </li>\n               <li>\n                Keep the soil moist until the fruit reaches full size then stop watering while the fruit ripens.\n               </li>\n               <li>\n                Mulch to retain soil moisture. Spread straw or dried chopped leaves around watermelon plants after the soil has warmed. You can also lay black plastic sheeting or garden fabric across the planting bed. Cut an x-slot in the fabric to plant.\n               </li>\n               <li>\n                Water at the base of plants with a soaker hose or drip irrigation. Wetting the foliage will leave plants susceptible to fungal diseases such as powdery or downy mildew.\n               </li>\n               <li>\n                Keep the soil evenly moist during flowering and fruit development. A week or so before harvest cut back on water; dry soil will help plants concentrate sugars in the fruit.\n               </li>\n               <li>\n                If leaves are wilted in the morning, the plants need water. Wilting leaves at the end of the day is not uncommon.\n               </li>\n              </ul>\n              <h2 id=\"h-feeding-watermelons\">\n               <span class=\"ez-toc-section\" class=\"ez-toc-section-end\">\n               </span>\n              </h2>\n              <ul>\n               <li>\n                Prepare planting beds with aged compost and aged manure or a commercial organic planting mix. Turn the soil to 12 inches (30cm) deep.\n               </li>\n               <li>\n                Watermelons are heavy feeders. Add several inches of aged manure across planting beds the autumn before planting.\n               </li>\n               <li>\n                Side dress watermelons with compost or manure tea or a dilute solution of fish emulsion every 2 to 3 weeks during the growing season.\n               </li>\n               <li>\n                Watermelons can be side dressed with an even organic fertilizer such as 10-10-10 early in the season but once flowers and fruit appear, reduce nitrogen and increase phosphorus and potassium; use a 5-10-10 fertilizer.\n               </li>\n              </ul>\n              <h2 id=\"h-companion-plants-for-watermelons\">\n               <span class=\"ez-toc-section\" id=\"Companion_Plants_for_Watermelons\">\n               </span>\n               <strong>\n                Companion Plants for Watermelons\n               </strong>\n               <span class=\"ez-toc-section-end\">\n               </span>\n              </h2>\n              <ul>\n               <li>\n                Plant watermelons with corn, radish, beans, and nasturtiums.\n               </li>\n              </ul>\n              <h2 id=\"h-watermelon-pollination\">\n               <span class=\"ez-toc-section\" id=\"Watermelon_Pollination\">\n               </span>\n               <strong>\n                Watermelon Pollination\n               </strong>\n               <span class=\"ez-toc-section-end\">\n               </span>\n              </h2>\n              <ul>\n               <li>\n                Watermelon plants produce both male and female flowers.\n               </li>\n               <li>\n                Male flowers appear a week or two in advance of the female flowers. Male flowers attract bees that are needed for pollination once female flowers appear.\n               </li>\n               <li>\n                Female flowers will have a small bulge at the stem end of the flower. This bulge is an embryonic fruit.\n               </li>\n               <li>\n                Some male flowers will die and drop before female flowers set fruit.\n               </li>\n               <li>\n                You can hand-pollinate watermelon flowers using an artist\u2019s bristle brush. Rub the brush in the male flower to collect pollen then rub the brush in the center of the female flower to transfer the pollen.\n               </li>\n               <li>\n                You can attract bees to your garden by planting flowering herbs such as dill, borage, and lavender nearby.\n               </li>\n              </ul>\n              <h2>\n               <span class=\"ez-toc-section\" id=\"Growing_Seedless_Watermelon\">\n               </span>\n               Growing Seedless Watermelon\n               <span class=\"ez-toc-section-end\">\n               </span>\n              </h2>\n              <ul>\n               <li>\n                Seedless watermelons are not truly seedless, though they look seedless. They contain nearly transparent seeds.\n               </li>\n               <li>\n                Seedless watermelons are known as triploid watermelons. They are a cross between a common diploid melon and a teraploid melon. The female flowers on a seedless plant must be pollinated by male flowers from a seeded variety. This process can be complicated for a home gardener.\n               </li>\n               <li>\n                Home gardeners can purchase seedless watermelon seed packets that will include the seed of a pollinator cultivar to be planted nearby.\n               </li>\n              </ul>\n              <div class=\"wp-block-image size-large wp-image-67852\">\n               <figure class=\"aligncenter\">\n                <img alt=\"Watermelon on wood to absorb sun\" class=\"lazyload wp-image-67852\" data-src=\"https://harvesttotable.com/wp-content/uploads/2009/03/bigstock-162738782-1-1024x683.jpg\" decoding=\"async\" src=\"data:image/svg+xml;charset=utf-8,%3Csvg xmlns%3D'http%3A%2F%2Fwww.w3.org%2F2000%2Fsvg' viewBox%3D'0 0 1 1'%2F%3E\"/>\n                <figcaption>\n                 Set the growing watermelon on a tile or wood. This will keep the melon off the ground and help it stay warm,\n                </figcaption>\n               </figure>\n              </div>\n              <h2 id=\"h-caring-for-watermelons\">\n               <span class=\"ez-toc-section\" id=\"Caring_for_Watermelons\">\n               </span>\n               <strong>\n                Caring for Watermelons\n               </strong>\n               <span class=\"ez-toc-section-end\">\n               </span>\n              </h2>\n              <ul>\n               <li>\n                Early in the season cover plants with a floating row cover. This will keep insects away and hold warm air around plants. Once plants begin to flower, remove the row cover during the day so that bees can get to the flowers.\n               </li>\n               <li>\n                Encourage watermelon plants to set three or four fruits at the same time; if a plant sets one fruit early, pinch it out to encourage the plant to develop several fruits at the same time. One fruit off to a head start can suppress all further fruiting on the vine until that fruit matures.\n               </li>\n               <li>\n                Cultivate carefully around vines until they cover the ground and smother out competing weeds.\n               </li>\n               <li>\n                Mulch around watermelons with straw, dry chopped leaves or set down black plastic mulch (sheeting) or garden fabric. Mulch will keep down weeds and conserve soil moisture.\n               </li>\n               <li>\n                For sprawling watermelons, place a tile, wooden shingle, or a piece of plastic under each melon to keep the fruit clean and dry and to store solar heat which will help ripen fruit.\n               </li>\n               <li>\n                About 50 days before the first expected autumn frost, remove all new blossoms from a plant; this will allow the plant to concentrate its energy on the development and ripening of fruit already on the plant.\n               </li>\n              </ul>\n              <p>\n               More tips:\n               <a href=\"https://harvesttotable.com/how-to-grow-watermelon-for-the-best-flavor/\">\n                <strong>\n                 How to Grow Watermelon for Best Flavor\n                </strong>\n               </a>\n               .\n              </p>\n              <h2 id=\"h-watermelon-pests\">\n               <span class=\"ez-toc-section\" id=\"Watermelon_Pests\">\n               </span>\n               <strong>\n                Watermelon Pests\n               </strong>\n               <span class=\"ez-toc-section-end\">\n               </span>\n              </h2>\n              <ul>\n               <li>\n                Aphids and spotted and striped cucumber beetles will attack melons.\n               </li>\n               <li>\n                Hose away aphids with a blast of water or pinch out infested foliage.\n               </li>\n               <li>\n                Hand-pick and destroy cucumber beetles promptly; they can transmit cucumber bacterial wilt to melons. You can also control cucumber beetles by spraying insecticidal soap, neem oil, or dusting with kaolin.\n               </li>\n               <li>\n                Organic pesticides such as horticultural oil or neem oil are best suited for controlling insect pests.\n               </li>\n              </ul>\n              <p>\n               More tips:\n               <a href=\"https://harvesttotable.com/melon_growing_problems_trouble/\">\n                <strong>\n                 Melon Growing Problems: Troubleshooting\n                </strong>\n               </a>\n               .\n              </p>\n              <h2 id=\"h-watermelon-diseases\">\n               <span class=\"ez-toc-section\" id=\"Watermelon_Diseases\">\n               </span>\n               <strong>\n                Watermelon Diseases\n               </strong>\n               <span class=\"ez-toc-section-end\">\n               </span>\n              </h2>\n              <ul>\n               <li>\n                Watermelons are susceptible to anthracnose, Alternaria leaf spot, bacterial wilt, and powdery and downy mildew.\n               </li>\n               <li>\n                Planting disease-resistant varieties when they are available and maintaining the general cleanliness and health of your garden will help cut down the incidence of disease.\n               </li>\n               <li>\n                Do not handle the vines when they are wet; this can spread fungal diseases.\n               </li>\n               <li>\n                If a plant does become infected remove it before it can spread the disease to healthy plants.\n               </li>\n               <li>\n                <strong>\n                 Anthracnose\n                </strong>\n                is a soil-borne fungal disease that can cause leaf spots, leaf drop, wilting, and sometimes death. Keep the garden clean and plant disease-resistant varieties. Remove diseased plants from the garden immediately.\n               </li>\n               <li>\n                <strong>\n                 Bacterial wilt\n                </strong>\n                which is spread by cucumber beetles can cause watermelon plants to suddenly wilt and die just as they begin to produce fruit. Control cucumber beetles as soon as they appear.\n               </li>\n               <li>\n                <strong>\n                 Fusarium wilt\n                </strong>\n                is a fungal disease that causes leaves to curl and drop and stems to curl. Add lime to the soil ahead of planting; avoid overwatering during the season.\n               </li>\n               <li>\n                <strong>\n                 Gummy stem blight\n                </strong>\n                is a fungal disease that causes leaves to yellow and die. This disease is hard to control; pull up plants and dispose of them in the trash. Rotate crops each year.\n               </li>\n               <li>\n                <strong>\n                 Powdery mildew\n                </strong>\n                and other fungal diseases can be prevented and slowed by spray-misting plants with compost tea or a solution of 1-part skim milk and 9-parts water.\n               </li>\n              </ul>\n              <div class=\"wp-block-image size-large wp-image-67857\">\n               <figure class=\"aligncenter\">\n                <img alt=\"Growing watermelon to harvest\" class=\"lazyload wp-image-67857\" data-src=\"https://harvesttotable.com/wp-content/uploads/2009/03/bigstock-The-Growing-Water-melon-In-The-103682399-1024x768.jpg\" decoding=\"async\" src=\"data:image/svg+xml;charset=utf-8,%3Csvg xmlns%3D'http%3A%2F%2Fwww.w3.org%2F2000%2Fsvg' viewBox%3D'0 0 1 1'%2F%3E\"/>\n                <figcaption>\n                 Limit water for a week in advance of the harvest to concentrate sweetness.\n                </figcaption>\n               </figure>\n              </div>\n              <h2 id=\"h-harvesting-watermelons\">\n               <span class=\"ez-toc-section\" id=\"Harvesting_Watermelons\">\n               </span>\n               <strong>\n                Harvesting Watermelons\n               </strong>\n               <span class=\"ez-toc-section-end\">\n               </span>\n              </h2>\n              <ul>\n               <li>\n                Watermelons will be ready for harvest 65 to 90 days after sowing depending on the variety.\n               </li>\n               <li>\n                When watermelons are ready to harvest vine tendrils will begin to turn brown and die off. If the tendrils are green the melon is not ripe.\n               </li>\n               <li>\n                You can also test for watermelon ripeness by rapping your knuckle against the melon; a ripe watermelon will make a dull hollow sound when thumped.\n               </li>\n               <li>\n                The soil side of a watermelon will turn from white to pale yellow when the fruit is ready for harvest.\n               </li>\n               <li>\n                Ripe fruit will have a sweet aroma at the stem end.\n               </li>\n               <li>\n                Limit water for a week in advance of the harvest to concentrate sweetness.\n               </li>\n               <li>\n                Watermelons on a single plant will all be ready for harvest over a two-week period.\n               </li>\n               <li>\n                Use a sharp knife or garden pruner to cut the watermelon away from the vine.\n               </li>\n               <li>\n                Watermelons do not continue to ripen off the vine.\n               </li>\n              </ul>\n              <h2 id=\"h-storing-and-preserving-watermelons\">\n               <span class=\"ez-toc-section\" id=\"Storing_and_Preserving_Watermelons\">\n               </span>\n               <strong>\n                Storing and Preserving Watermelons\n               </strong>\n               <span class=\"ez-toc-section-end\">\n               </span>\n              </h2>\n              <ul>\n               <li>\n                Watermelons will keep in the refrigerator for up to a week if not cut or sliced, but sweetness and flavor may diminish.\n               </li>\n               <li>\n                A cut watermelon will keep in the refrigerator for about 4 days. Wrap the melon tightly in plastic to prevent cold burn or dehydration.\n               </li>\n               <li>\n                It takes about 12 hours to chill a large watermelon.\n               </li>\n               <li>\n                Watermelons can be kept in a cool, moderately moist place for 2 to 3 weeks without refrigeration.\n               </li>\n               <li>\n                Melon flesh can be frozen and rinds can be pickled.\n               </li>\n              </ul>\n              <p>\n               More tips:\n               <a              </h3>\n              <h2>\n               <span class=\"ez-toc-section\" id=\"Watermelon_Frequently_Asked_Questions\">\n               </span>\n               Watermelon Frequently Asked Questions\n               <span class=\"ez-toc-section-end\">\n               </span>\n              </h2>\n              <p>\n               <strong>\n                Q: Should I start watermelons indoors?\n               </strong>\n              </p>\n              <p>\n               A: To ensure watermelons have enough warm and hot weather to ripen, start seeds indoors 3 to 4 weeks before the average date of the last frost. Plant seeds n peat pots or other biodegradable containers that can be set directly in the ground; watermelons don\u2019t like their roots to be disturbed at transplanting time.\n              </p>\n              <p>\n               <strong>\n                Q: When can watermelons be planted outdoors?\n               </strong>\n              </p>\n              <p>\n               A: You can transplant watermelons into the garden when temperatures average 60\u00b0F, even warmer is better. Protect young seedlings with hot caps or cloches. Plastic milk jugs with their bottoms cut out will work, but remember to remove them during the day if the sun is bright.\n              </p>\n              <p>\n               <strong>\n                Q: What\u2019s the best way to plant watermelon seeds?\n               </strong>\n              </p>\n              <p>\n               A: Sow seeds 1 inch deep in hills, two to four seeds per hill, 4 to 6 feet apart. Some bush varieties can be spaced closer together.\n              </p>\n              <p>\n               <strong>\n                Q: Can watermelons be grown on a trellis?\n               </strong>\n              </p>\n              <p>\n               A: Yes. To be certain the fruits don\u2019t break off, or pull the vines away from their support, create a little \u201chammock\u201d for them with nylon netting or pieces of cloth.\n              </p>\n              <p>\n               <strong>\n                Q: Can I grow watermelons in very dry regions?\n               </strong>\n              </p>\n              <p>\n               A: Yes, try this: create a depression 3 to 5 inches deep and 12 to 15 inches across. Sow seeds or set transplant halfway up the sides of the depression then train the vines away from the depression. Water in the depression and the water will wick up to the roots, and the vine trained away will not get muddy.\n              </p>\n              <p>\n               <strong>\n                Q: Can I grow watermelons in cool summer regions?\n               </strong>\n              </p>\n              <p>\n               A: In cooler climates, grow smaller \u201cice-box: varieties. Plant these smaller melons in the warmest spot in your garden. Use black plastic mulch to trap heat in the soil.\n              </p>\n              <p>\n               <strong>\n                Q: How much fertilizer do watermelons need?\n               </strong>\n              </p>\n              <p>\n               A: Give watermelons a complete organic fertilizer at planting ot transplanting time and again when plants are established and just starting to send out runners. Work the fertilizer into the soil in circular furrows 8 inches away from the plants.\n              </p>\n              <p>\n               <strong>\n                Q: Will watermelons need extra water?\n               </strong>\n              </p>\n              <p>\n               A: Watermelons need plenty of water, but they also must have good drainage. Keep the soil evenly moist. Planting on mounds will ensure good drainage.\n              </p>\n              <p>\n               <strong>\n                Q: How can I tell when a watermelon is ready to harvest?\n               </strong>\n              </p>\n              <p>\n               A: Rap the melon with your knuckle. If the sound is sharp and high, the melon is not ripe. If the sound is dull and hollow, the melon is ripe. When the tendril closest to the melon is alive and green, it is not ripe. When the tendril dries up and turns brown, the melon is ripe. You can also look for yellowing on the spot where the melon rests on the ground.\n              </p>\n              <h2 id=\"h-watermelon-varieties-to-grow\">\n               <span class=\"ez-toc-section\" id=\"Watermelon_Varieties_to_Grow\">\n               </span>\n               <strong>\n                Watermelon Varieties to Grow\n               </strong>\n               <span class=\"ez-toc-section-end\">\n               </span>\n              </h2>\n              <ul>\n               <li>\n                <strong>\n                 Icebox size watermelons:\n                </strong>\n                \u2018Early Midget\u2019 (65 days); Fruits are commonly 30 pounds or more.\n               </li>\n               <li>\n                The watermelon has a thick, solid rind with sweet, succulent flesh that can be pink, red, yellow, orange, or grayish-white.\n               </li>\n               <li>\n                Male and female flowers appear on the same vine.\n               </li>\n               <li>\n
